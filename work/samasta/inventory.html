MODULE · SAMASTA

Inventory built for real-world stock movement

A stock workflow that handles inward acceptance, outward issuing, manual additions, and transaction-ledger visibility across PHC and SHC. Designed to reduce “we had it yesterday” problems by making quantities, states, and responsibility explicit.

Problem

Inventory fails when “stock” is just a number without a story

At PHC/SHC level, stock is constantly moving: received from warehouse, issued to sub-centers, dispensed to patients, and sometimes added manually due to emergency purchase. If the UI doesn’t preserve the “why” behind a quantity change, teams lose trust in the number and revert to informal workarounds.

What the Inventory module must solve

  • Inward acceptance integrity: received vs indent counts, item-level accept, and clear completion.
  • Outward issuing with accountability: who issued, to whom, and whether it was received.
  • Operational stock states: in-stock, out-of-stock, few left, inactive.
  • Ledger visibility: make stock changes traceable via transactions (inward/outward/issued/purchase/manual).
  • Low-effort maintenance: search by name / code and bulk actions for high-volume lists.

Users

Role-based responsibilities shape what “stock” means

Inventory is not a single-user system. The module is designed around PHC and SHC roles where the same stock truth is shared, but actions are constrained by responsibility and facility context.

PHC: MO / Pharmacist

Reviews stock states, accepts inward deliveries, issues outward to SHC/SC, and maintains thresholds to prevent stockouts from becoming surprises.

SHC: CHO

Receives outward consignments, verifies items, updates local availability, and uses “few left” triggers as early warning for indenting.

Facility teams

Consume stock through OPD and outreach flows. Inventory must stay aligned with dispensing reality and not drift into “paper-only” land.

Admins (dashboard)

Needs aggregated visibility, not operational controls: stock status distributions, movement patterns, and exception monitoring.

Flow

Inventory modeled as movement: accept, issue, dispense, reconcile

The module breaks inventory into four operational flows that match how facilities actually work: inward acceptance from warehouse, outward issuing to another institute, dispensing/issuing to patients, and reconciliation via ledger.

Key states and transitions

  • Inward completion: accepted count makes partial vs complete deliveries visible.
  • Outward lifecycle: created → notified → received (or pending) prevents silent losses.
  • Few left triggers: thresholds move a medicine into “Few Left” automatically, so it’s an operational alert, not a memory test.

Design decisions

Designed for high-volume lists and low patience

1) Bulk acceptance, with item detail when needed

Inward flows support “Mark all items as received” for speed, while still allowing drill-in on item details (batch number, manufacturing and expiry) for validation.

2) Search by medicine code and name

Inventory lists are designed for scanning and retrieval, not browsing. Search is always present to avoid forcing users through long catalog scrolls.

3) Tabs represent operational states, not categories

In-stock / out-of-stock / few left / inactive are the fastest way to answer “what’s the problem right now” without making users interpret raw quantities.

4) Quantity changes require a mode

“Add quantity” isn’t just a number field. It’s tied to a reason (manual, purchase, received from PHC, issued to SC, issued to patients) so the ledger stays meaningful.

5) Ledger first, reporting second

The medicine detail view acts as the primary audit surface (transaction list + filters). Reports summarize, but the ledger explains.

RBAC as UX

Permissions protect stock truth and prevent “creative accounting”

Inventory actions have consequences. The UI makes permissions visible through entry points and available actions rather than hiding everything behind generic forms.

Boundary 01

Accept inward

Acceptance is restricted to authorized facility roles. It completes an inward record and moves it from “not received” to accepted.

Boundary 02

Issue outward

Outward requires institute selection and item selection with explicit quantities, then notifies the receiver to confirm receipt.

Boundary 03

Edit quantity

Quantity edits are treated as transactions with a reason. “Mark inactive” is gated so catalog doesn’t get corrupted casually.

Edge cases

Designed for partial deliveries, mismatches, and missing dates

Indent vs received mismatch

Inward list surfaces “Indent Items” and “Received Items” to make partial deliveries obvious, so reconciliation happens early, not after a stockout.

No inward found for selected date

Explicit empty states prevent “maybe it didn’t sync” confusion and make the next action clear.

Outward pending receipt

Outward records include a received date state (received vs pending). This avoids “it left us” becoming the end of the story.

No transactions

Medicine detail includes a “No transactions” state so ledger views don’t silently fail when the item is new or hasn’t moved yet.

Screens

Key surfaces in the Inventory module

These screens show inventory as a transaction-led system, not a static stock count. The UI separates receiving, issuing, and manual adjustments into explicit modes so every quantity change has an owner, a reason, and a timestamp. Ledger and reports then make reconciliation fast instead of argumentative.

System fit

Inventory connects operations: OPD prescribing, dispensing, and reporting

Inventory is not an isolated module. It supports OPD and outreach by keeping availability trustworthy. It also powers the admin dashboard with state distributions (out-of-stock vs few-left) and movement patterns (received vs issued).

Clinical workflows depend on stock truth

Prescriptions and dispensing are only safe when “left in stock” isn’t fiction. Inventory state tabs make shortages visible early.

Reporting surfaces patterns, not blame

Medicines dispensation reports summarize movement over a date range. The intent is to support planning and redistribution, not punish facilities for being busy.

Impact

Fewer stock surprises, faster reconciliation, clearer responsibility

  • Inward acceptance becomes verifiable through accepted counts and item-level confirmation.
  • Outward issuing becomes traceable through institute selection and received-state tracking.
  • Few-left thresholds convert “memory-based reordering” into an explicit alert mechanism.
  • Ledger views reduce time spent debating stock by showing how the number changed.

What’s next

Close the gaps where stock systems usually break

Batch and expiry-first flows

Make FEFO (first-expiry-first-out) practical by surfacing expiry risk in lists and guiding issuing decisions with batch context.

Exception queues

Add exception surfaces: partial inward, mismatched counts, pending outward receipt beyond threshold, and suspicious manual edits.

Smarter reorder support

Use consumption patterns (dispensed/issued history) to recommend minimum indenting quantities instead of relying on one-time manual thresholds.
