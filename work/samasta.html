CASE STUDY · SAMASTA

Designing PHC operations for Karnataka

Samasta is a facility application and dashboard that helps Primary Health Centre (PHC) teams complete day-to-day workflows across identity, OPD services, inventory, and outreach follow-ups. It was built by Khushi Baby for the Govt. of Karnataka, and designed to adapt to responsibilities and permissions across roles.

Problem

Day-to-day facility work is multi-role, high-volume, and permission-sensitive

Samasta was designed for PHC-level operations where multiple roles touch the same beneficiary record and where task ownership shifts across registration, OPD, dispensing, inventory movement, and outreach follow-ups. The core challenge was building workflows that stay fast for frontline use while remaining governed by role responsibilities.

Key design challenges

  • Role-based responsibility in shared workflows

    Different staff members contribute to one service journey. The UI needed clear boundaries between view, edit, and accountable actions.

  • Fast identity and correct handoffs

    Identity is the front door. The system needed quick search and a biometric option with explicit consent and reliable fallbacks.

  • Operational truth for medicines and supplies

    Inventory isn’t a report. It directly affects prescribing and dispensing. The interface needed ledger-grade traceability for inward/outward/issued movements.

  • Connectivity and device constraints are normal, not edge cases

    Workflows had to behave predictably under unstable connectivity and on low-end devices, without creating silent data loss or duplicate work.

  • Minimal training dependency

    Consistency across modules and predictable patterns mattered more than novel UI. Every screen had to be learnable through repetition.

The design focus was to keep workflows role-aligned, auditable where needed, and fast under real constraints.

My Role

Sr. UI/UX Designer | Project Lead

I led the end-to-end product design for Samasta across the facility app and dashboard, translating PHC workflows and role responsibilities into a scalable, consistent interface. I worked closely with engineering, PM, and program operations to align the experience with deployment constraints and governance needs.

My responsibilities included:

  • System IA and workflow architecture — structured cross-module flows (identity → OPD → inventory/dispense → outreach) to reduce handoff ambiguity.
  • Design system and reusable patterns — defined shared components and interaction rules for mobile and web to keep modules consistent.
  • Role-based UX mapping (RBAC as UX) — ensured navigation, visibility, and actions matched role responsibilities, not just backend permissions.
  • Cross-platform design — facility app patterns and dashboard structures designed for heavy operational use.
  • Engineering collaboration — partnered with devs to keep UI behavior, permissions, and edge-case handling aligned in implementation.
  • Constraint-driven decisions — optimized flows for throughput, shared devices, and connectivity variability typical to PHC settings.

I owned experience quality across modules, so the platform could evolve without fragmenting patterns or increasing training burden.

Users & Operational Context

PHC ecosystem with shared ownership across roles

Samasta supports PHC and sub-center workflows where service delivery depends on fast coordination across roles. Users differ not only in access, but in responsibilities, frequency of use, and accountability.

Primary user groups

Facility users (PHC)

Daily operational work: registration, OPD services, lab workflows, dispensing, and stock visibility.

  • Medical Officer (MO): clinical review, OPD decisions, approvals where governance requires it.
  • Staff Nurse: triage support, visit support tasks, follow-up coordination.
  • Lab Technician: diagnostics workflow, results capture and traceability.
  • Pharmacist: dispensing flow and accountability on issued medicines.

Needs: speed, clarity, low error rate, predictable navigation.

Sub-center users (SHC)

Local service delivery and coordination with field workers.

  • CHO: service delivery and coordination across SHC flows.
  • ANM: maternal/child health workflows and follow-ups.

Needs: task-focused flows, clear eligibility/state, minimal backtracking.

Field users (Village)

Field data capture and household-level follow-up execution via a field app.

  • ASHA: beneficiary data creation, household visits, outreach follow-ups.

Needs: quick capture, offline tolerance, and resumable workflows.

Admin and oversight users

Configuration and visibility: manage users, facilities, geography, and view operational stats.

Needs: reliable filters, safe operations, and clear accountability.

Operational realities

  • High throughput service delivery with frequent interruptions
  • Shared devices and quick handovers between staff
  • Connectivity variability, especially outside urban facilities
  • Longitudinal records where duplicates reduce trust
  • Sensitive identity and demographic data requiring careful UI boundaries

Design implications

  • Role-first navigation and progressive disclosure
  • Clear states for handoffs (pending, completed, received, issued)
  • Predictable patterns across modules to reduce training dependency
  • Explicit feedback for permissions, failures, and connectivity states

Design Approach

Role-aligned workflows, not isolated screens

The Samasta experience was designed as a connected system across facility and field contexts. The goal was to keep daily workflows fast for frontline users, while ensuring governance and accountability where actions carry operational risk.

System-first IA

Designed around shared entities and handoffs: Beneficiary → Visit/Encounter → Services → Transactions (dispense, inward/outward, follow-ups).

Role-first navigation

Navigation and actions are shaped by responsibilities, so users see only what they can act on and what they need to complete their tasks.

Handoff visibility

Used explicit states (pending/received/issued/completed) so ownership doesn’t disappear between roles and shifts.

Reuse over reinvention

Standardized list patterns, status tabs, action sheets, confirmations, and empty/offline states to reduce cognitive load and speed up delivery.

Constraint-driven decisions

Designed for throughput, shared devices, connectivity variability, and repetitive data entry without breaking user trust.

The result is a platform where modules feel consistent, responsibilities remain clear, and the system stays usable under real operating conditions.

Design System in Practice

Reusable patterns for heavy operational work

Samasta needed consistency across mobile and web while supporting different roles and data density. The design system focused on predictable interaction rules that scale across modules, not one-off screen styling.

Status-as-navigation

Used tabs and status buckets so users can triage quickly without deep filter dependency.

Governed actions via explicit entry points

Grouped sensitive actions behind clear affordances (menus, action sheets, confirmations) to reduce accidental edits.

Audit-friendly detail views

Where accountability matters (inventory movements, issued medicines), detail screens include traceable metadata and consistent logs/ledgers.

Offline + failure state patterns

Standardized empty states, loading, permission blocks, and connectivity messaging so users always know what happened and what to do next.

Cross-platform consistency

Kept core components and interaction rules consistent while allowing density and layout to adapt for dashboard use.

The system reduced relearning across modules and made it easier to ship additional workflows without fragmenting the experience.

RBAC as a UX Problem

Designing visibility and actions around responsibilities

In Samasta, role-based access isn’t just a backend rule. It affects navigation, what appears on lists, which actions are available, and which steps require confirmation. RBAC was treated as a first-class design constraint.

View vs action boundaries

Users may view shared records, but only specific roles can perform accountable actions like closing OPD, issuing medicines, accepting inward, issuing outward, or enrolling biometrics.

Permission-aware UI feedback

When actions are restricted, the UI avoids silent failures by removing controls or providing contextual explanation tied to role and context.

Auditability for high-risk actions

Inventory movement and identity actions produce traceable records so accountability survives across shifts and handoffs.

Consistency across modules

The same role rules behave consistently across identity, OPD, inventory, and outreach flows, reducing training dependency.

RBAC became a usability feature: it reduces clutter for frontline users and makes governance explicit for operational actions.

Constraints & Tradeoffs

Designing for real operating conditions

Samasta was designed for environments where constraints are routine: connectivity varies, devices are shared, and staff operate under time pressure. These realities shaped tradeoffs across modules.

Connectivity variability

Some workflows can tolerate sync delays; others require online validation (for example, certain identity lookups). The UI must make this boundary visible.

Tradeoff: Prefer predictable states and recovery paths over hidden retries.

Low-end device performance

Operational screens must stay lightweight and responsive.

Tradeoff: Conservative UI density and fewer heavy visual treatments.

Shared devices and handoffs

Work shifts between staff and roles; the system must keep task state and accountability clear.

Tradeoff: More explicit states and confirmations, less implicit automation.

Sensitive identity and demographic data

Biometric and identity flows require explicit consent, clear opt-out paths, and safe fallbacks.

Tradeoff: Added gating steps for trust and compliance.

Training realities

Many users learn systems through repetition, not manuals.

Tradeoff: Prioritized consistent patterns over role-specific reinventions.

Constraints were treated as design inputs, not exceptions, so the platform stays usable during daily facility operations.

Impact

Clearer ownership, safer actions, faster daily workflows

Samasta’s impact is primarily operational: it supports day-to-day execution with role-aligned visibility and reduces ambiguity at handoff points across identity, OPD, and inventory workflows.

Operational impact

  • Faster beneficiary entry through multiple search modes and a biometric option with explicit consent and fallbacks.
  • Better continuity between facility and field work through structured follow-ups and outreach handoffs.
  • More accountable stock movement through inward/outward/issued traceability and consistent states.

User experience impact

  • Reduced cognitive load through consistent patterns across modules.
  • Clearer guardrails for permissions and sensitive actions.
  • More predictable behavior under connectivity constraints through explicit failure states.

Product and delivery impact

  • Reusable patterns reduced rework as additional workflows and modules evolved.
  • Shared UI rules made handoffs between engineering and design clearer for permission and state handling.

The system is structured to grow in features without fragmenting behavior, keeping the experience stable across roles.

What’s Next

Extending the platform without breaking patterns

Samasta was designed as a modular system where new workflows can be added without rewriting navigation, permission rules, or interaction patterns. The next evolution is less about adding features and more about strengthening governance and operational clarity at scale.

Tighten role-action governance

Expand action-level guardrails and audit trails for high-risk operations (inventory adjustments, biometric changes, encounter closure), so accountability remains clear across shifts and facilities.

Improve exception-led oversight

Push dashboard patterns toward exception queues (stock-outs, pending inward, incomplete encounters, overdue follow-ups), so supervisors spend less time filtering and more time resolving.

Make offline boundaries explicit

Standardize offline capability by workflow (offline OK / hybrid / online required), and reflect it in UI copy, save states, and recovery paths to reduce uncertainty during connectivity drops.

Keep modules consistent as they grow

Continue investing in shared components, patterns, and UI rules so module teams don’t diverge. Consistency is the actual scaling strategy.

The goal is to scale Samasta’s capability while keeping the experience predictable for frontline users and governed for oversight roles.
