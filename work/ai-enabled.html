CASE STUDY · AI-ENABLED DASHBOARD

Designing an AI-enabled performance command center for public health governance

This AI-enabled automated and integrated dashboard consolidates multi-program public health indicators into a single performance view across state → district → block geographies. It supports ABP cohort monitoring, rankings, drilldowns, and time-based analysis, and closes the loop from insight to action through governed workflows like auto-generated letters, WhatsApp/push messaging, and polls and surveys. A role-scoped LLM copilot enables natural-language questions over the same curated datasets without exposing unauthorized data or actions.

Problem

Public health performance monitoring is fragmented, slow to act on, and hard to govern

Program reviews relied on scattered MIS exports, inconsistent indicator definitions, and manual follow-ups across state and district teams. Leaders could see problems, but the system could not consistently answer: where is the gap, what changed over time, and what action was taken. The design challenge was to unify indicators, geography, and time into a single operational surface, while keeping governance controls (RBAC, communication permissions, and audit trails) explicit.

Key design challenges

  • One indicator model across many programs

    Domains and indicators needed a consistent taxonomy and naming so users could compare districts without debating definitions or duplicating dashboards per program.

  • Geography drilldowns at scale

    State → district → block navigation had to be fast, predictable, and comparable (including ABP block cohorts) without losing context or creating filter chaos.

  • Rankings that remain explainable

    Scores, ranks, and “top/bottom” views needed transparent inputs and time windows so users could trust what they were seeing and defend decisions in review meetings.

  • Closing the loop from insight to action

    Workflows like letters, WhatsApp, push notifications, and surveys required governed templates, recipient scoping, and an audit trail so actions were consistent and reviewable.

  • RBAC across data, actions, and admin tooling

    Uploads and master data management could not sit beside everyday monitoring without clear permission boundaries, scope-based visibility, and safe defaults for non-admin roles.

The design focus was to keep monitoring comparable, actions governed, and outcomes traceable across reviews.

My Role

Senior UI/UX Designer (L3)

I led the platform UX for the integrated dashboard: defining the information model (indicator × geography × time), designing drilldown and ranking patterns, and translating governance needs into role-scoped actions (letters, comms, surveys) with audit-friendly states. I worked closely with engineering, program ops, and government stakeholders to ensure the UI reflected real administrative boundaries and operational accountability.

My responsibilities included:

  • Platform IA and navigation model — defined Dashboard, Explore, Tables, Analytics, Fact Sheets, and action surfaces so users could move from overview to drilldown without losing context.
  • Indicator and geography framework — structured domain/indicator taxonomy, ABP cohort segmentation, and state → district → block scoping rules that powered filters and comparisons.
  • RBAC as interface behavior — designed scope-aware visibility and action gating for messaging, exports, uploads, and master data so permissions were legible (not “backend magic”).
  • Action workflows — designed templates and flows for letters, WhatsApp/push messaging, and polls/surveys with recipient scoping, previews, and confirmation steps.
  • AI copilot UX (LLM bot) — designed prompting, guardrails, and answer presentation so natural-language analysis stayed within the user’s data scope and remained verifiable.
  • Design system patterns for dense data — standardized filter bars, ranking lists, drilldown tables, exports, and empty/loading states for consistency and performance.

I owned end-to-end experience quality across monitoring, analysis, and action so the platform stayed usable under real governance and operational constraints.

Users & Operational Context

Multi-level review workflows with clear scope ownership and controlled actions

The dashboard supports performance reviews where roles differ in authority and scope: state users compare districts, district users drill into blocks, and operations teams execute follow-ups. Users do not just “view data”. They are accountable for acting on it, which makes permissions, audit trails, and repeatable patterns essential.

Primary user groups

State leadership and program owners

State-wide monitoring, prioritization, and cross-program review.

  • Mission leadership and program heads: interpret trends, compare districts, and define focus areas for reviews.

Needs: trustworthy rankings, fast drilldowns, and consistent views across programs.

District and block officials

Local performance review and follow-up execution within a bounded geography.

  • District teams: identify low-performing blocks and track improvements over time.
  • Block teams: validate on-ground issues and respond to escalations from review meetings.

Needs: clarity on “why this is low”, what changed, and what to do next.

Data and MIS teams

Data readiness, definitions, and issue resolution across sources.

  • MIS and analytics users: validate indicator pipelines, handle corrections, and support review cycles with exports.

Needs: clear definitions, consistent computation, and reliable exports.

Operations and communication admins

Execute actions and track follow-ups from review decisions.

Needs: role-scoped recipient lists, templates, previews, and an audit trail for outbound communications.

Operational realities

  • Monthly and quarterly review cadences with shifting focus priorities
  • Data lag and mismatch risk across multiple MIS sources
  • ABP cohort monitoring alongside full-state comparisons
  • High-pressure follow-ups (letters/comms) with accountability expectations
  • Mixed device and connectivity conditions for district/block users

Design implications

  • A single information model: Indicator × Geography × Time as the primary navigation spine
  • Scope-first visibility (state/district/block) so users see only what they’re accountable for
  • Governed action surfaces with templates, recipient scoping, and confirmation steps
  • Audit-friendly trails: what was sent, when, by whom, and linked to which indicator/geography

Design Approach

A repeatable monitoring-to-action workflow for governance at scale

The platform was designed as a repeatable workflow: start with a performance overview, drill into a specific indicator and geography, validate trends over time, then execute a governed action. The UI needed to stay fast for routine monitoring while making the “take action” layer explicit and permission-safe.

Indicator × Geography × Time model

Built filters and drilldowns around a single mental model so users could move across programs without relearning navigation.

Progressive disclosure from overview to evidence

Used rankings and top/bottom views to surface priorities, then tables and charts to explain what changed and where.

ABP cohort as a first-class segment

Made ABP block views comparable to “all blocks” views so aspirational tracking could happen without creating a separate product.

Governed action workflows

Designed action flows (letters, WhatsApp/push, polls) as structured steps with templates, previews, and confirmations, tied to the selected indicator and geography.

Role-scoped AI copilot

Designed the bot to answer within the user’s scope, show the basis of answers, and avoid suggesting actions the user cannot perform.

The platform keeps monitoring comparable while making follow-ups governable, auditable, and repeatable.

Design System in Practice

Reusable patterns for dense data, drilldowns, and follow-up actions

The dashboard reuses a small set of dense-data patterns so behavior stays predictable across domains and roles. The design system focus was consistency in filters, drilldowns, and action affordances, even as indicator sets and cohorts changed.

Unified filter bar

Standardized geography, time window, and cohort filters to reduce rework and prevent “filter state surprises” in reviews.

Ranking + drilldown tables

Kept a consistent “compare then drill” pattern across districts and blocks, with clear baselines and exports.

Trend-first analytics views

Time-series charts and period selectors were standardized so users could validate deltas before escalating.

Action drawer patterns

Action menus open a structured flow (template → recipient → preview → confirm) tied to the current context.

Export and share primitives

Downloads and fact sheets follow consistent placement, labeling, and states so teams can rely on them during meetings.

Consistent patterns reduce relearning, improve trust, and make governance workflows repeatable across programs.

RBAC as a UX Problem

Making scope and authority visible through interface states and actions

In this platform, RBAC is not just about hiding menu items. It defines what data a user can view, which geographies they can drill into, and which actions they are allowed to initiate. The interface makes these boundaries visible so users understand scope, authority, and accountability.

Scope-based visibility

State users see state-wide comparisons; district users see their districts and blocks; block users see only their local scope, without dead-end filters.

Action permissions as explicit affordances

Messaging, letters, surveys, uploads, and master data are gated through visible controls and explanatory states rather than silent failures.

Recipient scoping and safety

Communication actions inherit the selected geography and role scope so users cannot broadcast outside their authority.

AI copilot within RBAC

The bot answers only from accessible datasets and avoids producing “state-wide” insights for district-scoped users.

RBAC becomes a usability feature: fewer dead ends, safer actions, and clearer ownership.

Constraints & Tradeoffs

Designing for governance, trust, and performance at scale

The platform had to behave like an operational system, not a presentation dashboard. Data quality and lag, governance requirements, and performance constraints shaped the tradeoffs across monitoring, analytics, and action workflows.

Data lag and trust

Indicators update at different cadences and sources can disagree.

Tradeoff: Prioritized clear “time window” controls and definitions over pretending everything is real-time.

Performance at scale

Large tables, many indicators, and deep drilldowns are expensive.

Tradeoff: Used progressive disclosure and predictable exports instead of loading everything upfront.

Governance for outbound actions

Letters and messaging carry accountability and reputational risk.

Tradeoff: Added structured templates and confirmations over free-form broadcasting.

Explainability for scores and ranks

Rankings are only useful if they’re defensible in reviews.

Tradeoff: Kept computation inputs visible rather than optimizing only for visual simplicity.

AI guardrails

Natural-language querying can overreach scope or produce uncheckable answers.

Tradeoff: Preferred scoped prompts and verifiable outputs over “magic” responses.

Constraints were treated as design inputs so reviews remain reliable and follow-ups stay governed.

Accessibility & Inclusive Design

Accessibility & Inclusive Design

The interface was designed to support mixed digital comfort through clear hierarchy, consistent patterns, and reduced reading burden where possible. Language readiness was considered through simple terminology, shorter copy blocks, and repeatable labels that can be localized without changing workflow meaning. Readability was aimed for with legible type, comfortable tap targets, and contrast-aware components that keep primary actions and critical states easy to scan. Error prevention focused on inline validation, specific error messaging, safe defaults, and confirmation for risky actions, with reversible states or undo patterns where applicable.

Constraints addressed

Constraints addressed

These constraints were treated as design inputs rather than edge cases. The patterns below show how the UI supports dense monitoring, interruptions, and governance requirements without turning the platform into a fragile “filter maze”.

Dense data + scanning

  • Hard to find what matters → top/bottom views, ranks, and consistent “why” drilldowns.
  • Context loss on drill → sticky filter model and clear indicator/geography breadcrumbs.
  • Trust in rankings → visible time windows, definitions, and explainable inputs.

Governance + safety

  • Role confusion → scope-first visibility and gated actions with explanatory states.
  • Risky outbound comms → templates, previews, confirmations, and audit trails.
  • Admin bleed into daily use → clear separation of monitoring vs admin tooling and safer defaults.

Interruptions + handoffs

  • Meeting-driven context switches → fast re-entry through stable entry points and predictable surfaces.
  • Follow-ups slip → action flows tied to indicator/geography, with history and traceability.
  • Back-and-forth across tools → monitoring-to-action workflow inside the same platform context.

Notes on measurement

Notes on measurement

Measurement focused on practical workflow outcomes: time to reach key answers in reviews, task completion on drilldowns and exports, common error types (mis-scoped filters, confusing time windows), and action follow-through for letters/comms/surveys. Evidence came from stakeholder walkthroughs, meeting observations, and pilot feedback, supported by basic analytics/log review where available (for example, exports used, retries, and action history). Some metrics are presented as proxies/estimates where instrumentation was limited.

  • Time-to-answer for common review questions (where is the gap, what changed, why this district)
  • Drilldown and export completion rates for high-frequency monitoring flows
  • Common error types: filter mis-scopes, time-window confusion, and missing context on comparisons
  • Follow-up throughput: actions initiated, previews used, and traceability of outbound comms

Impact

Faster review cycles through comparable monitoring and governed follow-ups

Impact is measured in operational clarity: faster review cycles, fewer manual exports, and more consistent follow-ups. The system makes it easier to move from “we saw a gap” to “we acted and can trace it.”

Operational impact

  • More consistent review meetings through comparable rankings and drilldowns across programs.
  • Reduced follow-up latency by moving letters/comms into the same context as analysis.
  • Improved accountability via auditable “who did what” records tied to indicator + geography.

User experience impact

  • Lower cognitive load through one navigation model repeated across domains.
  • Fewer dead-ends through scope-first filters and RBAC-visible actions.
  • Higher trust through time-window clarity and explainable rankings.

Product and delivery impact

  • Reusable patterns enabled faster onboarding of new indicator sets and cohorts.
  • Clear permission boundaries reduced rework when new admin or comms features were added.

The platform is structured to scale monitoring and follow-ups without sacrificing governance or trust.

What’s Next

Strengthening follow-through and verification without bloating the platform

The next phase focuses on strengthening action follow-through, improving anomaly detection, and making the AI layer more verifiable, without expanding the platform into an ungovernable “do everything” tool.

Add follow-up tracking to actions

Link letters/comms to outcomes (responses, status changes, next review notes) so escalation is not a one-way broadcast.

Exception-based triage

Introduce anomaly flags (sudden drops, missing data, extreme deltas) so teams spend time on exceptions instead of scanning everything.

ABP improvement views

Make delta trends and improvement rankings more prominent for ABP blocks so progress is measurable beyond absolute scores.

Stronger AI verification UX

Add “show basis” affordances (sources within the platform, applied filters, and time windows) so copilot answers are easy to validate.

The goal is to keep governance strong while making reviews faster, more repeatable, and easier to act on.
