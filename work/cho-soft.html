CASE STUDY · CHO SOFT

Designing incentive governance for Rajasthan SHCs

CHO Soft is a mobile app + dashboard bundle that enables activity-wise incentive claiming, verification, and payment processing for AAM facilities at the Sub Health Centre (SHC) level in Rajasthan. The system is designed around role responsibilities across CHO claim entry, MO/MOIC verification, and BCMO payment approval, with mandatory proof uploads, policy thresholds, and irreversible payment initiation.

Problem

Incentive processing is multi-role, policy-bound, and audit-sensitive

CHO Soft was designed for SHC operations where incentives are tied to activity performance, mandatory evidence, and policy thresholds. The core challenge was building a workflow that stays fast for frontline users while remaining governed by role responsibilities, deadlines, and irreversible payment processing.

Key design challenges

  • RBAC across a single claim lifecycle

    One monthly claim moves across CHO → MO/MOIC → BCMO. The UI needed clear boundaries between who can edit, who can verify, and who can approve for payment.

  • Policy-legible amounts and thresholds

    Users needed to understand why an amount was expected, why a claim was reduced or rejected, and which threshold blocked submission or approval without relying on offline clarifications.

  • Mandatory proofs without slowing the system

    Evidence requirements differ by activity. The experience had to enforce mandatory uploads while remaining usable on low-end devices and unstable networks.

  • Clean correction loops (rejected-only resubmission)

    Rejections had to be actionable and limited to rejected activities only, keeping approved items stable and audit trails coherent.

  • Deadlines and irreversible payment initiation

    Monthly windows and a ‘point of no return’ at payment initiation required clear status visibility, confirmations for high-risk actions, and predictable handoffs.

The design focus was to keep the workflow role-aligned, policy-legible, and auditable at payment time.

Research Summary

Making monthly incentive cycles role-clear, policy-legible, and proof-complete

Research centered on the roles that touch the same monthly claim: CHOs entering activities and proofs, MO/MOIC verifying against thresholds, and BCMO approving and initiating payment, with program ops and policy reviewers influencing the rules. Work happens under monthly deadlines with policy thresholds, mandatory proof uploads, and low-end devices with unstable connectivity across SHC/PHC settings. The biggest pain points were unclear ownership and editability as claims moved across states, and repeated rework caused by missing proofs or policy ambiguity discovered late in the cycle. Insights shifted the direction toward a state-driven workflow, RBAC expressed in visible UI boundaries, completeness gates, structured rejection reasons with rejected-only resubmission, and guarded irreversible payment initiation.

Methods

  • Interviews with CHO and verification staff, plus supervisory and ops stakeholders
  • Workflow walkthroughs of the monthly claim cycle (draft → submit → verify/reject → approve → payment initiated)
  • Contextual inquiry/shadowing during claim preparation, proof capture, and verification
  • Usability testing on prototypes with a focus on proof upload and correction loops
  • Review of policy documents and operational feedback (escalations/support patterns)
I keep fixing the same claim, because proofs or reasons aren’t clear.

How insights shaped the design

The workflow needed to stay fast for CHOs while remaining review-safe and audit-ready for verification and payment. Design decisions focused on making state, ownership, and policy constraints visible at the moment of action so corrections are actionable and handoffs stay predictable.

  • Role confusion across states → explicit state labels, action visibility by role, and locked fields once submitted
  • Missing proofs discovered late → completeness gates, mandatory proof rules, and clear upload state feedback on low connectivity
  • Rejection churn → rejected-only resubmission, structured reasons, and a stable audit trail across the monthly cycle

Users & Operational Context

SHC incentive workflow with clear role ownership and approvals

CHO Soft supports an SHC-level incentive and target-verification workflow where multiple roles touch the same monthly claim. Users differ not only in access, but in accountability: who enters performance, who verifies evidence, and who approves payment.

Primary user groups

Verification users (PHC)

Claim verification and governance at PHC level.

  • Medical Officer (MO/MOIC): verifies submitted claims against proofs and policy thresholds; can reject with reasons.

Needs: fast review, clear evidence access, and consistent rejection/approval patterns.

Claim owners (SHC)

Monthly claim preparation, proof upload, and resubmission.

  • CHO: updates every activity in the monthly list, uploads mandatory proofs, submits to MO, and resubmits rejected activities only.
  • ANM: provides activity targets used for expected amount computation.

Needs: task-focused entry, clear submission readiness, and minimal backtracking.

Field users (Village)

Data inputs that influence targets and mismatch checks.

  • ASHA: survey and field updates that can surface achievement mismatches during review.

Needs: reliable capture and sync so downstream verification doesn’t fail silently.

Approval and payment users

Final approval and payment execution across geographies.

Needs: geo filters, status visibility, deadline monitoring, and safe irreversible payment actions.

Operational realities

  • Monthly deadlines for submission, verification, and approval
  • Policy thresholds that can block submission or reduce payouts
  • Mandatory proofs per activity with variable requirements
  • Mismatch risk due to multi-source data (field surveys, facility registers)
  • Low-end Android + unstable connectivity during proof uploads and sync

Design implications

  • Role-first visibility and action boundaries across a shared claim object
  • Explicit claim states and timestamps to support audit and handoffs
  • Submission gating (all activities updated + mandatory proofs)
  • Rejected-only correction loops to prevent version sprawl

Design Approach

State-driven workflow design across mobile and dashboard

CHO Soft was designed as a single claim lifecycle expressed across two surfaces: mobile for entry and verification, and dashboard for approvals and payment initiation. The goal was to enforce policy rules without turning the system into a training-heavy checklist.

Claim state machine

Defined Draft → Submitted → Verified/Rejected → Approved → Payment initiated so permissions and editability remain consistent at every step.

Completeness gates

Submission requires every activity in the list to be updated and mandatory proofs to be attached where required, reducing verifier back-and-forth.

Role-aligned action surfaces

CHO edits and submits on mobile, MO/MOIC verifies on mobile, and BCMO approves and initiates payment on the dashboard with geo filters and deadlines.

Correction without churn

Rejections return only rejected activities to an editable state for CHO, keeping approved items stable and audit trails coherent.

Irreversible payment initiation

BCMO’s payment initiation is explicitly irreversible to match treasury/bank workflows and prevent retroactive edits after processing starts.

The system keeps incentives policy-legible for frontline users while remaining auditable for approvals and payment processing.

Design System in Practice

Reusable patterns for monthly incentive cycles

CHO Soft extends CHIP 2 patterns to support predictable behavior across a monthly incentive cycle. The design system focus was consistency in status navigation, review actions, and audit-friendly detail views across mobile and dashboard.

Status-as-navigation

Used status buckets to triage claims quickly across Draft, Submitted, Verified, Rejected, and Approved states.

Activity cards for policy context

Standardized activity summaries (Target, Achieved, Expected, Claimed) so users can interpret payouts without policy documents open in parallel.

Governed actions via confirmations

Used confirmations for verify/reject and payment initiation to reduce accidental high-impact actions.

Audit-friendly detail views

Detail views prioritize evidence visibility, timestamps, and consistent metadata so review decisions remain explainable later.

Cross-platform consistency

Kept interaction rules consistent while allowing density and filtering to adapt for BCMO dashboard review.

This reduced relearning across roles and made it easier to scale monthly cycles without fragmenting behavior.

RBAC as a UX Problem

Making RBAC visible through interface states and actions

In CHO Soft, RBAC is expressed directly in the interface: who can edit a claim, who can verify it, and who can initiate payment. These boundaries reduce clutter for frontline users and protect auditability at approval time.

Edit vs review boundaries

CHO is the only role that can edit monthly activity entries; MO/MOIC and BCMO review and act without mutating entered data.

Shared evidence visibility

Proof documents remain visible to CHO, MO/MOIC, and BCMO so decisions are traceable across handoffs.

Role-specific critical actions

MO/MOIC verifies or rejects; BCMO performs final approval and initiates payment. High-risk actions are guarded by confirmations and irreversible transitions.

Permission-aware feedback

Controls are removed or disabled based on role and claim state to avoid silent failures and reduce training dependency.

RBAC becomes a usability feature: fewer wrong actions, clearer ownership, and safer monthly processing.

Constraints & Tradeoffs

Designing for policy compliance and operational constraints

CHO Soft was designed for operational conditions where policy compliance and auditability matter as much as usability. Deadlines, mandatory proofs, and multi-role approvals shaped the tradeoffs.

Deadlines and cycle closure

Monthly windows for submission, verification, and approval require the UI to make readiness and pending work obvious.

Tradeoff: Prioritized clear states and reminders over flexible late submissions.

Mandatory evidence uploads

Proofs are required per activity, which increases friction but reduces rejections and disputes later.

Tradeoff: Chose upfront compliance gates over downstream correction burden.

Low-end device + unstable networks

Uploads and sync must remain predictable to prevent duplicate work or lost evidence.

Tradeoff: Preferred explicit feedback and retry patterns over hidden retries.

Mismatch risk from multi-source data

Field inputs and facility registers can conflict, creating review friction.

Tradeoff: Made mismatches reviewable rather than auto-resolved to avoid silent policy violations.

Irreversible payment initiation

Once BCMO initiates payment, reversals are operationally expensive.

Tradeoff: Added confirmation friction to protect auditability and reconciliation.

Constraints were treated as design inputs so monthly processing remains predictable, explainable, and governed.

Accessibility & Inclusive Design

Accessibility & Inclusive Design

The interface was designed to support low-literacy use with icons paired with clear labels, reduced reading burden, and progressive disclosure so the first view stays scannable. Language readiness was built in through simple, consistent terminology and copy that avoids dense paragraphs, making localization easier without changing core workflow logic. Readability was aimed for through clear hierarchy, legible type, comfortable tap targets, and contrast-aware components that separate primary actions from supporting information. Error prevention focused on inline validation, specific error messaging, safe defaults, and confirmation for risky actions, with reversible states or undo patterns where applicable.

Constraints addressed

Constraints addressed

These constraints were treated as design inputs, not edge cases. Patterns below show how the UI was shaped to keep work moving with fewer errors, even when attention, connectivity, or time were limited.

Low literacy

  • High reading burden → icons paired with labels and consistent placement for primary actions.
  • Hard-to-scan screens → short, structured content with progressive disclosure for details.
  • Ambiguous outcomes → clear status labels and success messaging (saved, pending, submitted).

Low connectivity

  • Unclear offline state → visible connection cues and explicit “saved locally” feedback.
  • Sync failures and retries → queued sync with retry controls and non-blocking error states.
  • Duplicate submissions → duplicate-safe submission flows and safer retry behavior where possible.

High-volume data entry

  • Too much typing → sensible defaults, smart prefills, and reuse of previously captured values.
  • Slow navigation across steps → lightweight task-based flows with predictable next actions.
  • Late error discovery → inline validation and immediate feedback on required fields.

Notes on measurement

Notes on measurement

Measurement focused on practical workflow outcomes: task time, completion, common error types, and evidence/data completeness across the monthly cycle. Evidence came from field observation and pilot feedback (including interruptions and cross-role review), supported by basic analytics and log review where available (for example, save vs submit, retries, and upload states). Some metrics are presented as proxies/estimates where instrumentation was limited.

  • Task time and step count on high-frequency flows (monthly activity entry, proof upload, submission)
  • Review friction signals (rejections, reason patterns, and resubmission frequency)
  • Common error types: missing required data, missing proofs, and threshold-related submission blocks
  • Completeness checks at submission and review (required fields and evidence visibility)

Impact

Cleaner monthly cycles through role-aligned incentives and auditability

CHO Soft’s impact is operational: it reduces ambiguity in monthly claim processing by making responsibilities explicit and embedding policy context into the workflow.

Operational impact

  • Cleaner monthly cycles through state-driven submission, verification, and approval handoffs.
  • Lower churn through rejected-only resubmission instead of full-month rework.
  • Safer payment execution via irreversible payment initiation and exportable payment files.

User experience impact

  • Reduced cognitive load by making expected vs claimed amounts legible per activity.
  • Fewer errors through mandatory proof gates and submission readiness checks.
  • More predictable review through consistent approve/reject patterns.

Product and delivery impact

  • Reusable patterns enabled faster iteration as policy rules evolved.
  • Clear RBAC mapping reduced implementation rework across mobile and dashboard.

The platform is structured to scale approvals without sacrificing auditability or frontline usability.

What’s Next

Extending CHO Soft without breaking the monthly cycle

CHO Soft was designed to extend without breaking the monthly cycle. Next steps focus on strengthening policy clarity, review quality, and payment reconciliation at scale.

Improve rejection quality and reuse

Refine rejection reasons into a policy-aligned taxonomy and introduce structured mismatch diagnostics so CHOs can correct issues faster and reviewers stay consistent.

Strengthen payment audit and reconciliation

Add payment file history, included claim references, and reconciliation states so BCMOs can trace what was paid and resolve disputes without offline spreadsheets.

Reduce verifier load via exception triage

Introduce anomaly flags (missing proofs, threshold failures, large deltas) so MO/MOIC and BCMO spend time on exceptions rather than scanning every activity.

Improve connectivity resilience for proofs

Strengthen upload feedback and retry states for mandatory proofs so users don’t repeat work or lose submission windows under unstable networks.

The goal is to scale monthly processing while keeping the workflow predictable for frontline users and governed for approval roles.
