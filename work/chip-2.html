Designing End-to-End Program Operations

CHIP 2.0 digitizes workflows across public health programs and facilities , enabling consistent execution in the field and clearer visibility for program oversight.

Problem

UI scaling and no RBAC failed

As CHIP expanded across programs, states, and partner organizations, the existing product architecture began to show structural cracks. What worked for a smaller deployment became increasingly fragile at scale.

Key problems included:

  • Inconsistent user experience across modules

    Similar tasks behaved differently in DHS, Family Planning, and other modules, forcing users to relearn flows and increasing errors.

  • High cognitive load for frontline users

    Field workers were exposed to unnecessary information and options, slowing down task completion in time-sensitive, real-world conditions.

  • Duplication in design and development

    Lack of a shared design system and role-aware patterns meant new features were repeatedly redesigned and rebuilt for different roles.

  • Poor performance on low-end devices and networks

    Heavy screens, long forms, and unclear offline states made the system unreliable in rural and low-connectivity environments.

  • Limited scalability for new states and programs

    Adding a new module or onboarding a new state required disproportionate effort due to fragmented UI patterns and rigid workflows.

Together, these issues increased training dependency, slowed rollouts, and reduced confidence in the system’s ability to scale sustainably.

My Role

Lead UI/UX Designer | Systems & Product Design

I led the end-to-end design of CHIP 2.0, owning the product experience across modules, platforms, and user roles. Working closely with engineering and program stakeholders, I translated complex healthcare workflows and RBAC requirements into a scalable, usable system.

My responsibilities included:

  • End-to-end product design — defined user flows, interaction models, and visual design across all healthcare modules, from frontline task execution to administrative oversight.
  • Design system ownership — built a unified design system for mobile and web, enabling consistency, reuse, and faster rollout of new modules and states.
  • Role-based UX architecture — designed interfaces that adapt to different roles, permissions, and data depth so each user sees only what’s relevant.
  • Cross-platform design — responsive experiences for Android and web, optimized for low-end devices, varying screen sizes, and unreliable connectivity.
  • Collaboration with engineering — aligned RBAC logic with UI behavior, component visibility, and action-level permissions to reduce ambiguity.
  • Constraint-driven design — balanced usability with low digital literacy, offline usage, government workflows, and program-mandated task structures.
  • Mentorship and quality control — mentored a design intern, reviewed work, and enforced system patterns and design principles.

I acted as the single point of design ownership, ensuring CHIP 2.0 could scale without sacrificing usability or consistency.

Users & Operational Context

Multi-layered public healthcare ecosystem

CHIP 2.0 operates within a multi-layered public healthcare ecosystem where users differ not only in access levels, but in responsibilities, frequency of use, and decision-making authority. The platform serves both frontline health workers operating in the field and administrative users managing programs at scale.

Primary user groups

Frontline & Facility-Level Users

These users interact with the system daily and are responsible for data collection, service delivery, and on-ground execution.

  • ASHA: household visits, surveys, follow-ups, data entry, often offline.
  • ANM & CHO: manage clinical workflows, validate data, track compliance, oversee ASHAs.
  • Medical Officer (MO): reviews data, approves actions, monitors facility performance, ensures adherence.
  • PHC Staff: facility operations, reporting, cross-program coordination.

Needs: speed, clarity, error prevention, minimal cognitive load.

Supervisory & Administrative Users

Oversight, planning, and performance monitoring across geographies and programs.

  • Block & District Officials: monitor targets, track progress, identify gaps, take corrective actions.
  • State-Level Users: analyze trends, evaluate program impact, make policy/planning decisions.
  • NGO & Partner Administrators: oversee implementation, manage access, ensure data quality across deployments.

Needs: data aggregation, comparability, actionable insights over task execution.

Operational realities

  • Unreliable internet; offline-first interactions and clear sync feedback
  • Low-end Android devices; performance-aware UI and lightweight components
  • Varying digital literacy, especially among frontline workers
  • High-stakes data entry affecting incentives, reporting, outcomes
  • Multi-state deployments with different workflows and governance
  • Role, facility, and responsibility switching over time

Design implications

  • Role-aware without being role-heavy
  • Flexible enough to support diverse workflows
  • Strict where compliance and data integrity mattered
  • Forgiving where real-world constraints demanded it

This understanding directly informed decisions around navigation, component behavior, information hierarchy, and role-based interaction patterns throughout the platform.

Design Approach

Design for scale, reality, and roles

The design approach for CHIP 2.0 was driven by one core principle: design for scale, reality, and roles, not for ideal conditions or isolated screens. Every decision had to hold up across modules, states, devices, and user types.

System-first, not screen-first

Treated the product as a connected ecosystem; shared workflows and patterns were foundations, not one-offs. New modules slot in without reinventing navigation or interaction logic.

Role-aware by design

  • Task-focused views for frontline workers
  • Summaries and exceptions for supervisory roles
  • Progressive disclosure based on responsibility level

Constraint-driven decisions

  • Low digital literacy and offline/poor connectivity
  • Older Android devices, performance-aware UI
  • Long, repetitive data entry; program-mandated structures

Favored clarity, predictability, and error prevention over novelty.

Mobile-first, cross-platform

Designed frontline workflows for small screens and limited attention first; extended patterns to web for supervisory/admin users without forcing mobile constraints onto desktop.

Reuse over reinvention

Standardized forms, approvals, validations, status indicators, and patterns to reduce cognitive load and speed up design/development for new modules.

Collaboration & iteration

Continuous validation with engineering and program stakeholders; field realities and rollout needs shaped iterations to keep design intent aligned with implementation.

By focusing on systems, roles, and constraints, CHIP 2.0 evolved into a platform that could grow in complexity without becoming harder to use—enabling faster scaling, clearer ownership, and a more resilient foundation.

Design System in Practice

A living system for scale and clarity

The design system was built as a shared foundation for a role-driven healthcare platform across mobile and web—focused on scalability, clarity, and operational reliability over decoration.

Foundations for heavy workflows

Typography, spacing, and color tuned for prolonged use and dense data. Conservative spacing keeps long forms readable; color remains functional for status and feedback.

Components for variation

Forms scale from quick capture to multi-step workflows with validation, partial completion, and offline states. Cards/lists surface critical info first, with progressive detail.

Status indicators handle eligibility, approval, sync, and errors with states for loading, disabled, restricted, and offline.

Patterns that reduce errors

Standardized interactions: long tasks split into resumable steps; intentional confirmations for irreversible actions; consistent offline rules with clear saved/pending/synced states.

Role-aware components

Same component adapts by role—editable for field, limited updates for supervisors, read-only for admins—permissions drive behavior while visuals stay consistent.

Cross-platform consistency

Core components and interaction logic align across Android and web; density, navigation, and layout adapt per platform so familiarity remains without forcing identical UIs.

Reuse over reinvention

Standardized forms, approvals, validations, and indicators reduced effort to add modules or onboard states, turning decisions into reusable assets instead of one-offs.

The design system became a living framework aligning design, development, and operations—making CHIP 2.0 easier to build, use, and extend without accruing design debt.

RBAC as a UX Problem

Designing for intent, visibility, and trust

RBAC was treated as a core UX challenge, not an afterthought. Multiple roles with different authority and data sensitivity shaped interaction, visibility, and cognitive load—not just backend permissions.

Designing for intent

Interfaces centered on what each role does most often: frontline users see task-driven flows; supervisors see overviews, exceptions, and trends; admins manage configuration and access.

Visibility by design

Information density, component visibility, and navigation structure shift by role. Users aren’t burdened with out-of-scope data or actions, reducing overload and misuse.

Action-level control & feedback

Actions like edit, approve, assign, or escalate carry clear affordances tied to role and context. When unavailable, the UI responds with immediate, contextual feedback—no silent failures.

Data depth & navigation

Field users work at record/task depth; supervisors move between summaries and drill-downs; state-level users stay in aggregated dashboards. Hierarchy and drill depth align with responsibility.

Consistency across modules

RBAC behavior stays predictable across DHS, Family Planning, and other programs, reducing relearning and increasing trust as users move between modules.

Backend alignment

Close alignment with backend roles, facilities, and hierarchies kept permission logic and UI behavior in sync, reducing ambiguity and role-specific UI bugs.

RBAC became an enabler of clarity and trust: users see interfaces matched to their responsibilities, developers work with a clearer permission model, and the system scales without added friction.

Constraints & Tradeoffs

Designing within real-world limits

CHIP 2.0 was designed inside structural and environmental constraints—these were everyday realities, not edge cases. Each limitation shaped specific tradeoffs to keep the platform usable and reliable.

Low digital literacy

Interfaces had to be immediately understandable, forgiving, and consistent for time-sensitive field work.

Tradeoff: Advanced customization and dense controls were avoided in favor of predictable patterns and guided workflows.

Unreliable connectivity

Users often worked offline for long stretches.

Tradeoff: Real-time interactions and instant validations were delayed or simplified to ensure reliability offline/low-bandwidth.

Low-end Android devices

Older devices with limited memory/CPU dominated frontline use.

Tradeoff: Visual polish and micro-interactions were deprioritized for performance, clarity, and faster load times.

Long, repetitive data entry

Extensive, multi-step forms carried fatigue and error risk.

Tradeoff: Structured, sometimes rigid workflows reduced errors even if they limited edge-case flexibility.

Mandated processes

Government and program rules dictated task structures.

Tradeoff: Flows weren’t radically restructured; instead they were clarified, made less error-prone, and easier to resume.

Role complexity & hierarchy

Users switched roles/facilities and worked in layered hierarchies.

Tradeoff: Some actions were intentionally hidden rather than explained to reduce overload and risk.

Multi-state variability

Each state/partner had different operational needs.

Tradeoff: Prioritized configurable patterns over bespoke UI; some optimizations handled operationally, not through divergence.

Design vs delivery pressure

Continuous delivery alongside system improvements.

Tradeoff: Accepted some legacy constraints and non-ideal workflows to keep rollouts on time.

By acknowledging constraints upfront, CHIP 2.0 balanced usability with feasibility—favoring long-term scalability and reliability over short-term perfection.

Modules

What we shipped across programs

CHIP 2.0 spans multiple healthcare programs, designed to reuse patterns while respecting unique workflows, data depth, and role needs.

Onboarding (Login, Registration)

Onboarding that treats identity + posting as governance.

A role-aware login and registration flow that establishes identity, posting location, and approval state upfront. The UI prevents duplicate registrations, makes “pending approval” a first-class state, and routes approved users into a day-start surface so work begins without hunting through menus.

Attendance & Homepage

Day-start that grounds work in place, context, and status.

A role-aware day-start system that makes punch-in a real operational state, not a polite suggestion. Users select where work is happening (facility, village, outreach, VHND, monitoring, meetings), see execution counters and pending governance work, and get a clean “start visit” path without menu hunting.

ABHA

ABHA workflows that hold up in the field.

A field-ready ABHA flow inside CHIP 2.0 that supports search, verification, creation, and updates using Aadhaar OTP / Mobile OTP, explicit consent capture, and online/offline paths.

View module

Family Register & DHS

Geo-scoped Family Register with DHS capture gates.

A field-first register that lets frontline workers discover houses and families by area, keep family + member profiles updated, and run DHS-style capture flows with “presence”, GPS, and photo gates. The UI is built to handle daily realities: incomplete data, updates required, duplicates, and interruptions.

View module

mDash - DHS Overview

Mobile dashboard for ABHA + DHS progress, across admin levels.

A drill-down “progress + attention” surface for supervisors to track registration and DHS completion from State → District → Block → PHC/CHC → SHC → Village/Area. It supports time windows (All time / Today / This week / This month), demographic splits, and verification queues so action is obvious without exporting reports.

View module

Sickle Cell & IMR/MMR

Sickle Cell screening + IMR/MMR household capture, built as governed UI states.

A field-ready set of DHS-adjacent flows that capture: (1) Sickle Cell screening status, DBS sample collection, barcode validation, and follow-up needs, and (2) household-level infant and maternal death event capture with structured, repeatable “individual details” steps. The design treats follow-up and data integrity as primary outcomes, not side effects.

View module

Family Planning

Family Planning that behaves like governance, not a form.

A role-aware Family Planning module built around eligible couple tracking, due follow-ups, and event-based status changes (notified → confirmed → closed). It supports frontline realities: low attention, interruptions, mixed connectivity, and strict program rules.

View module

Maternal Health

Maternal Health: ANC, risk, tests, and outcomes as one operational loop.

A role-aware maternal health module designed for frontline delivery where due lists, checkup capture, risk flags, lab completion, and pregnancy outcome must stay connected. Instead of treating ANC as “a form”, the UX is built as a predictable state machine that keeps cases moving across ASHA → ANM and facility handoffs.

View module

GoK Directory

GoK Directory: find the right person, at the right level, without calling 5 people first.

A role-aware directory that maps District → Block → PHC → SHC → Village chains, and organizes contacts across health workers, elected members, and committee roles. The UX is designed for field operations: fast calling, clear ownership, and governance-aligned edits.

View module

Death Notification and Confirmation

Death notifications as a governed state change.

A role-aware workflow to notify, validate, and confirm a death without turning it into a “one-tap delete.” The module treats death as a high-integrity system event: it is routed for confirmation, classified by age/sex logic, and only then permanently removes a beneficiary from programs.

View module

VHSNC Meeting

VHSNC Meetings as a governed execution workflow.

A village-level planning, conduct, and verification system where “meeting happened” is treated as a trackable state machine. The UI supports role-based responsibility (ASHA executes, supervisors verify), evidence capture, and scalable rollups across SHC and PHC geographies.

Impact

Scale without added friction

CHIP 2.0 is actively deployed across multiple states and partner organizations, growing in users, programs, and complexity without a corresponding increase in usability issues or operational overhead.

Operational impact

  • Scaled multi-state deployments (e.g., Maharashtra, Karnataka, Rajasthan) and partners like PATH.
  • Simplified role management at scale with unified RBAC-driven UX.
  • Faster rollout of new modules/programs via shared components and patterns.

User experience impact

  • Reduced cognitive load for frontline workers through role-focused interfaces.
  • Improved learnability across modules with standardized patterns.
  • Increased confidence via clear feedback for offline states, permissions, and completion.

Product & engineering impact

  • Lower design/dev overhead through the design system.
  • Better alignment between UI behavior and RBAC logic, reducing bugs/rework.
  • More maintainable architecture that supports ongoing iteration.

Indicative metrics (portfolio-safe)

  • Reduction in role-specific UI variations across modules
  • Faster onboarding of new states and programs
  • Decrease in role-related usability issues post-rollout
  • Improved task completion clarity reported by field users

Reflected in consistent qualitative feedback from program teams, engineers, and frontline users.

Long-term value

CHIP 2.0 established a sustainable design and UX architecture that can evolve with changing programs, policies, and technologies—without requiring fundamental redesign.
